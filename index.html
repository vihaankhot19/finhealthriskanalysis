Methodology & Documentation

Full technical description, assumptions, and limitations for portfolio and academic use.

Overview

This application models household financial dynamics using Monte Carlo simulation — a statistical technique that estimates the probability distribution of outcomes by running thousands of randomized scenarios. Rather than projecting a single "expected" future, we generate an entire distribution of possible futures and summarize it with percentiles, risk metrics, and descriptive statistics.

Simulation Engine

Monthly Cash-Flow Model

Each month in each simulation run, the following cash-flow identity is applied:

Net Flow = Income − Fixed Expenses − Variable Expenses − Debt Payment − Extra Investment

The cash balance at the end of the month is:

Cash[t] = Cash[t-1] + Net Flow[t]

If Cash[t] < 0, the shortfall is drawn from the investment/savings pool before declaring "ruin."

Income Shock Model

Monthly income is drawn from a normal distribution:

Income[t] ~ N(μ_income, σ_income)

where σ_income = μ_income × (volatility% / 100). The Box-Muller transform is used to generate Gaussian samples from uniform random numbers. Income is floored at $0 to prevent negative income.

Variable Expense Model

Variable expenses are drawn from N(μ_var, 0.25 × μ_var) with a floor at 20% of the mean, representing irreducible minimum spending.

Inflation

Fixed and variable expenses are scaled monthly by (1 + r_inflation)^t / 12 to reflect real purchasing-power erosion over time.

Debt Dynamics

Outstanding debt accrues interest monthly at APR / 12. Three payment strategies define the three scenarios:

  • Minimum Payment: Pay exactly the minimum required each month.
  • Aggressive Payoff: Pay 2.5× minimum toward debt each month, accelerating principal reduction and interest savings.
  • Invest Surplus: Minimum payments only; once debt reaches $0, redirect 10% of monthly income to investments.

Investment Growth

The investment/savings pool grows monthly at a rate drawn from N(r_annual/12, |r_annual/12| × 0.4 + 0.005). The small additive noise term (0.005) prevents degenerate zero-variance when the return rate is near 0.

Statistical Methods

Descriptive Statistics

  • Mean, Median, Standard Deviation: Standard moment-based estimators using N−1 (Bessel-corrected) variance.
  • Skewness: Fisher-Pearson standardized third moment — positive values indicate a right-heavy tail (more upside extreme outcomes).
  • Excess Kurtosis: Standardized fourth moment minus 3 (so the normal distribution scores 0). Positive values indicate heavier-than-normal tails ("fat tails").
  • Percentiles: Linear interpolation between adjacent order statistics.

Value-at-Risk (VaR)

We use the historical simulation (non-parametric) approach: sort the 5,000 ending net-worth values and take the appropriate quantile as the VaR threshold. VaR at 95% answers: "In 95% of scenarios, your ending net worth is no worse than X." Expected Shortfall (CVaR) is the mean of outcomes in the worst 5%.

Correlation Analysis

Pearson correlation coefficients are computed between the end-of-horizon values (net worth, cash, savings, debt) across sampled trajectories. This reveals structural relationships in the simulation output — for example, high ending debt is typically strongly negatively correlated with ending net worth.

OLS Regression

A simple ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is fitted to the median net worth time series. The slope β gives the average monthly net-worth growth in the central scenario; R² measures how well a linear trend explains the path (compounding nonlinearity reduces R²).

Assumptions & Limitations

Key Assumptions

  • All inputs are after-tax; the model does not simulate tax brackets or capital gains taxes.
  • Income shocks are i.i.d. (independently and identically distributed) — no autocorrelation. Real income often shows serial correlation (raises, layoffs).
  • Market returns are drawn from a stationary normal distribution. Real markets exhibit fat tails, skewness, and regime changes (e.g., 2008 financial crisis).
  • Expense growth follows deterministic inflation compounding — no modeling of lifestyle changes, emergencies, or health costs beyond the variable-expense distribution.
  • Debt is a single pooled instrument with a weighted average APR — individual loan tranches are not modeled separately.

Limitations

  • This is a simplified educational model, not a certified financial planning tool.
  • The model does not account for Social Security, pensions, inheritance, large one-time expenditures, or home equity.
  • Survivorship bias: "ruin" simulations are terminated at first cash default but the run still contributes to the ending distribution as if recovery were instant.
  • With 5,000 runs and a 30-year horizon (~360 months), computational limitations reduce trajectory storage to a random sample of 200 runs for the confidence-band visualization.
